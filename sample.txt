The old mill stood at the edge of the village, where the river bent twice before
running on toward the sea. Every morning the miller woke before the sun and
walked down the stone path to open the great wooden doors, and every morning
the swallows that nested under the eaves scattered into the grey light.

The children of the village liked to sit on the low wall across the river and
watch the wheel turn. They said that if you watched it long enough you could
learn to tell time without a clock, just by counting how many times the wheel
dipped into the water while the kettle boiled at home. Nobody had ever proved
this, but nobody had ever disproved it either, and so it remained one of the
small true things that everybody in the village believed.

In the autumn the wheat came down from the high fields in carts pulled by two
brown horses named Pepper and Ash. The miller's apprentice, a quiet boy named
Tomas, learned to judge the grain by rubbing it between his fingers and
smelling it, the way his master had taught him. Good grain smelled like warm
bread even before it was ground. Bad grain smelled like nothing at all, and
that was somehow worse than smelling bad.

Tomas kept a small notebook where he wrote down the weather each day, and the
weight of the carts, and anything strange that happened at the mill. Most days
there was nothing strange to report. But he wrote anyway, because his master
had told him that a miller who pays attention is a miller who is never
surprised, and a miller who is never surprised never ruins the flour.

One winter the river froze solid enough to walk on, which had not happened in
eleven years. The wheel stopped turning and the whole village came down to
look at it, hanging there silent and white with frost. The miller told Tomas
that the wheel was resting, the way a person rests, and that come spring it
would turn again as if nothing had happened. Tomas wrote this down in his
notebook too, underlined twice, because it seemed like the kind of thing worth
remembering.

When spring came the ice broke apart in great grey slabs that floated down the
river and knocked gently against the mill's stone foundation. The swallows
came back first, then the carts, then the smell of warm bread drifting out
over the water in the early morning, before anyone in the village was awake
to notice it.
