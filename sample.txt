Chapter One: The Old Mill

The old mill stood at the edge of the village, where the river bent twice before
running on toward the sea. Every morning the miller woke before the sun and
walked down the stone path to open the great wooden doors, and every morning
the swallows that nested under the eaves scattered into the grey light.

The children of the village liked to sit on the low wall across the river and
watch the wheel turn. They said that if you watched it long enough you could
learn to tell time without a clock, just by counting how many times the wheel
dipped into the water while the kettle boiled at home. Nobody had ever proved
this, but nobody had ever disproved it either, and so it remained one of the
small true things that everybody in the village believed.

In the autumn the wheat came down from the high fields in carts pulled by two
brown horses named Pepper and Ash. The miller's apprentice, a quiet boy named
Tomas, learned to judge the grain by rubbing it between his fingers and
smelling it, the way his master had taught him. Good grain smelled like warm
bread even before it was ground. Bad grain smelled like nothing at all, and
that was somehow worse than smelling bad.

Tomas kept a small notebook where he wrote down the weather each day, and the
weight of the carts, and anything strange that happened at the mill. Most days
there was nothing strange to report. But he wrote anyway, because his master
had told him that a miller who pays attention is a miller who is never
surprised, and a miller who is never surprised never ruins the flour.

One winter the river froze solid enough to walk on, which had not happened in
eleven years. The wheel stopped turning and the whole village came down to
look at it, hanging there silent and white with frost. The miller told Tomas
that the wheel was resting, the way a person rests, and that come spring it
would turn again as if nothing had happened. Tomas wrote this down in his
notebook too, underlined twice, because it seemed like the kind of thing worth
remembering.

When spring came the ice broke apart in great grey slabs that floated down the
river and knocked gently against the mill's stone foundation. The swallows
came back first, then the carts, then the smell of warm bread drifting out
over the water in the early morning, before anyone in the village was awake
to notice it.

Chapter Two: The Merchant from the Coast

Two summers after the river froze, a stranger rode into the village on a mule
loaded with sacks and led another mule besides. He wore a coat too heavy for
the weather and spoke with an accent that curled the ends of his words in a
way Tomas had never heard before.

"I am looking for the mill," the stranger said, though the wheel was turning
in plain sight behind him. "I was told the flour here is the finest between
the hills and the harbor."

The miller wiped his hands on his apron and studied the man for a long moment
before answering. "Told by whom?"

"By everyone who has eaten bread made from it," the stranger said, and smiled
in a way that made Tomas uncertain whether to trust him. "My name is Alaric. I
buy grain and flour for the ships that leave the harbor town, three days east
of here. I pay in coin, not in promises."

The miller considered this. Coin was rare in the village; most trade was done
in favors and grain and the occasional chicken. "Show me your coin, then," he
said, "and I will show you my flour."

Alaric laughed, a short, surprised sound, and reached into his coat. What
followed was an afternoon of careful bargaining, the kind Tomas had only read
about in his master's old ledgers. By evening the stranger had bought twelve
sacks of flour and promised to return each autumn if the quality held.

"Watch him closely, Tomas," the miller said after Alaric had ridden off with
his mules groaning under their new weight. "A man who pays well the first time
is testing whether you will still deserve it the second."

Tomas wrote this down too, though he was not yet sure what it meant.

Chapter Three: The Dry Summer

The summer Tomas turned sixteen, no rain fell from the middle of June until
the first week of September. The river that turned the wheel dropped so low
that the miller had to wade out with a shovel to clear stones from the
millrace, muttering the whole time about years he remembered his own master
describing, years before Tomas was born, when the wheel had stopped turning
altogether and the whole village had ground grain by hand for a season.

"Will it come to that?" Tomas asked, standing on the bank with a bucket he had
been sent to fill and had mostly failed to.

"Not if the wheel and I have anything to say about it," the miller said, which
was not really an answer, but Tomas had learned that his master often spoke
that way when he did not want to frighten anyone, including himself.

They worked the wheel in short bursts that summer, running it hard when the
river gave them enough water and resting it the rest of the time, the way a
person paces themselves through a long, hot day of work. Tomas took to
sleeping near the millrace some nights, listening for the particular gurgle
that meant the water had risen enough to be worth waking the miller for.

It was Tomas, in the end, who woke his master at three in the morning in
early August, insisting he had heard the river's voice change. The miller
grumbled his way down to the bank, listened, said nothing for a full minute,
and then clapped Tomas on the shoulder harder than he ever had before.

"You have the ear for it now," he said. "That is not a thing I can teach. I
can only wait for it to arrive, the way I waited for my beard to grow in."

Tomas did not write that one down. He did not need to. Some things, he was
beginning to understand, stayed with you whether or not you wrote them in a
notebook.

Chapter Four: Pepper and Ash Grow Old

The horses Pepper and Ash had pulled the grain carts down from the high
fields for as long as anyone in the village could remember, and by the time
Tomas had been the miller's apprentice for five years, both animals had gone
grey around the muzzle and slow in the mornings.

"They have earned a quiet field," the miller said one day, watching Pepper
lean into the harness with visible effort. "We will find younger horses this
autumn, and let these two spend their last years eating grass instead of
pulling carts."

Tomas felt an unexpected tightness in his chest at this, though he tried to
hide it, since it seemed a foolish thing to feel about horses that were not
even his own. The miller noticed anyway; he noticed most things, eventually.

"It is not weak to be fond of a good animal," the miller said. "It would be
strange not to be, after this many years of good work between you."

They bought two younger horses at the autumn market, a pair of dark bays that
startled at the sound of the wheel for their first few weeks and gradually
learned to ignore it, the way every new thing at the mill eventually learned
to be unremarkable. Pepper and Ash were given the field behind the mill
house, where Tomas visited them most evenings with an apple or a handful of
oats, and where, three winters later, Pepper died quietly in his sleep, and
Ash followed him within the month, as though he had simply decided there was
no reason to stay once his old companion had gone.

The miller helped Tomas bury them both beneath the same old oak tree, and
neither of them spoke much that day, and Tomas did not write anything in his
notebook that night at all.

Chapter Five: The New Apprentice

When Tomas was one-and-twenty, a boy from the eastern edge of the village
named Willem came asking for work, the way Tomas himself once had. The
miller, whose hands had begun to ache in cold weather more than they used to,
looked at Tomas rather than at Willem when he asked whether they had room for
another set of hands.

"That is not really my decision to make," Tomas said, though the way the
miller kept looking at him suggested it was becoming exactly that.

"It will be, before long," the miller said. "Better you start deciding now,
while I am still here to tell you if you decide wrong."

So it was Tomas who showed Willem how to judge good grain from bad by smell
and touch, Tomas who explained the sound the river made when it was rising,
Tomas who handed over, on Willem's third week, the very notebook he had kept
since he was a boy, so that Willem could start one of his own instead of
guessing at things that had already been carefully written down once.

"Why give this away?" Willem asked, turning the worn pages over with visible
care.

"Because I do not need it written down anymore," Tomas said, and found, a
little to his own surprise, that this was true. "It is yours now. Add to it.
Someday you will hand it to someone else, and it will be twice as long, and
half of it will be things I never thought to notice."

Chapter Six: The Storm

In Tomas's twenty-fourth year, a storm came up from the sea faster than
anyone in the village had ever seen weather move, and by the time the sky had
finished turning the color of a bruise, half the roof of the mill house had
lifted away like a lid and the millrace was running brown with churned mud
and broken branches.

The miller, older now and slower on the ladder than he once was, stood in the
rain shouting instructions that the wind mostly tore away before they reached
anyone, while Tomas and Willem worked to lash down what they could and clear
debris from the wheel before it jammed and cracked under the strain of
turning against a branch the size of a doorframe.

They lost a third of the roof and two sacks of flour that had been stacked
too near a window, but the wheel itself held, and by the time the storm had
passed on toward the hills, the three of them sat exhausted and soaked on the
mill house steps, watching the clouds break apart to reveal a sky scrubbed
unusually clean and bright.

"We will need the whole village to help rebuild that roof," the miller said.

"They will come," Tomas said, with more certainty than he might have felt a
few years earlier. "They always do, for the mill."

And they did come, carpenters and farmers and the merchant Alaric's cousin
who happened to be passing through with a delivery, all of them climbing
ladders and passing boards hand to hand for three days until the roof was
whole again, better in places than it had been before the storm arrived to
break it.

Chapter Seven: A Quiet Winter

The winter after the storm was unusually quiet, the kind of winter where
nothing much happens and everyone is secretly grateful for it. The river froze
only partway, the wheel turned slower than usual but never stopped, and
Willem filled the first third of his new notebook with observations about
frost patterns on the millrace that made the old miller laugh out loud when
he read them, not unkindly, but with the particular fondness of someone
recognizing his own younger self in someone else's careful handwriting.

Tomas spent much of that winter teaching Willem the parts of the work that
could not be written down at all: how to tell, by the particular note the
wheel made as it turned, whether a bearing needed grease before it needed
replacing; how to read the miller's silences, which came in at least four
different varieties, only one of which meant anything was actually wrong;
how to wait, when waiting was the only useful thing left to do.

"Your master taught you all this?" Willem asked one grey afternoon, watching
frost patterns form on the inside of the mill house window.

"Some of it," Tomas said. "Some of it I only learned by getting it wrong
first, the way you will too. That part cannot be taught, only survived."

Chapter Eight: What the Wheel Remembers

Years later, when Tomas's own hair had begun to grey and his own hands had
begun to ache in the cold the way his master's once had, he would sometimes
stand at the low wall across the river, the very spot where he had sat as a
boy trying to tell time by counting the wheel's dips into the water, and
watch some new apprentice hurry back and forth across the millrace on
errands that felt, to that apprentice, entirely new and unprecedented.

The old miller had been gone eleven years by then, buried near the oak tree
beside Pepper and Ash, exactly as he had asked. The mill itself looked much
the same as it always had: the same stone foundation the ice had once
knocked against, the same great wooden doors, the same swallows returning
each spring to nest beneath the same eaves, though surely, Tomas thought, not
the very same swallows.

"Does the wheel get tired?" a child once asked him, sitting on the wall the
way children always had, the way Tomas himself once had.

"No," Tomas said, after considering it seriously, because the question
deserved to be considered seriously. "But it remembers. Every storm, every
dry summer, every winter it rested under the ice. It carries all of that in
how it turns now, even if it cannot tell you the stories the way I can."

The child seemed satisfied with this answer, the way children often are
satisfied with answers that are mostly true, and went back to counting the
wheel's dips into the water, learning to tell time the way every child in the
village eventually learned to, whether or not it was ever proved, whether or
not it needed to be.

Chapter Nine: The Festival of First Bread

Every year, on the first Sunday after the spring wheat was cut, the village
held what it called the Festival of First Bread, though by Tomas's time
nobody could say for certain how old the custom was or who had started it.
Long tables were carried out into the square in front of the chapel, and
every household brought a loaf made from that year's earliest flour, and the
miller's family, by tradition, brought three loaves instead of one.

"Why three?" Willem's daughter asked one year, tugging at Tomas's sleeve. Her
name was Elin, and she had inherited her father's habit of asking questions
that seemed simple until you tried to answer them.

"One for the mill," Tomas said, counting on his fingers the way his own
master once had. "One for the river, since the river turns the wheel. And
one for whoever is hungriest that day, whoever they might be."

"That is not a real tradition," Elin said, narrowing her eyes at him. "You
are making that up."

"Perhaps I am," Tomas admitted. "But your father tells it the same way, and
his master told it to him the same way before that, so at some point a thing
that is made up becomes true simply by being repeated enough times by
enough people who wanted to believe it."

Elin considered this with the particular seriousness of a child deciding
whether an adult was being foolish or wise, and appeared, in the end, unable
to decide, which Tomas privately thought was probably the correct
conclusion.

The festival went on late into the evening that year, with fiddles brought
out from the tavern and children chasing each other between the tables,
and Alaric's grandson, who had taken over the coastal trade route by then,
raised his cup to the miller's family and declared, rather too loudly, that
no bread between the hills and the harbor had ever come close to matching
it, and that he intended to say so at every table he sat at for the rest of
his life.

Chapter Ten: Willem's Daughter

Elin grew up the way the mill itself seemed to grow, slowly and almost
without anyone noticing the change until it had already happened. By twelve
she could judge grain as well as her father could, and by fifteen she had
taken to correcting Tomas on small points of the notebook's older entries,
insisting that a passage written forty years earlier about frost patterns
was, in her considered opinion, simply wrong.

"Your master was guessing," she told him, not unkindly. "Frost does not
follow the wind the way he wrote. It follows where the stone is coldest,
and the stone is coldest wherever the water splashes highest against it.
I have watched three winters running to be sure."

Tomas found he had no argument against three winters of careful watching,
and said so, and added a note in the margin of the old entry in his own
now-unsteady hand: Elin says otherwise, and has watched more closely than I
ever did. Trust her observation over mine.

"You should not write that," Elin said, reading over his shoulder. "It makes
you sound as though you did not know what you were doing."

"I did not, not always," Tomas said. "That is exactly the sort of thing this
notebook is for. If it only ever recorded the things we got right, it would
be a very short notebook, and not a very useful one to whoever reads it
after us."

Elin took over the notebook formally the winter she turned eighteen, the way
Tomas had taken it from the miller and the miller, presumably, from someone
before him, back through a chain of hands none of them could fully trace
anymore.

Chapter Eleven: The Letter from the Harbor

One autumn a letter arrived at the mill by a messenger none of them
recognized, sealed with wax the deep red color of the harbor merchants'
trading house. Tomas, whose eyes had grown poor enough that he now asked
Elin to read most correspondence aloud to him, sat by the window while she
broke the seal.

"It is from Alaric's grandson," she said, scanning the careful, slanted
hand. "He writes that the harbor town wishes to buy flour every month
instead of only in autumn, and asks whether the mill can grind enough to
meet it."

Tomas was quiet for a long moment, turning the question over the way he
once turned grain between his fingers, testing it for what it truly was
before answering. "Read me the last line again."

"He writes: my grandfather always said your mill's flour tasted like a
promise that had actually been kept, and I have never once, in all my years
of trade, found another to compare."

"Write back," Tomas said, "and tell him yes, we can meet it, so long as he
understands that some months the river gives us less to work with than
others, and that we will never grind more than the wheel can properly bear,
not even for a harbor town's coin."

Elin wrote exactly that, in her own hand now rather than her father's or
Tomas's, and the mill began grinding flour for the coast every month from
that year onward, though never, as promised, more than the wheel could
properly bear.

Chapter Twelve: The Wheel Turns On

Long after Tomas himself had been laid to rest beneath the same old oak
tree, near the miller and Pepper and Ash, Elin would sometimes bring her own
apprentices down to the low wall across the river and tell them the whole
long history of the mill, from the miller who first taught a quiet boy to
judge grain by smell, through the storm that tore away half a roof, through
the dry summer and the frozen winter and the festival where three loaves
were carried out instead of one for reasons nobody could fully prove.

"Is all of that true?" one apprentice asked, the way every apprentice
eventually asked, standing where Tomas had once stood asking the very same
question of someone else.

"Every word of it is written down," Elin said, patting the thick, many-
handed notebook that had grown heavier with each generation that added to
it. "Whether that makes it true, I could not tell you for certain. But it
has been carried this far, carefully, by people who believed it was worth
carrying, and that has always seemed like a kind of truth to me, whatever
the notebook itself might say."

The wheel turned on behind them, the same wheel, worn smoother now by all
the years between the first page of the notebook and the last, dipping into
the water again and again, keeping whatever time it had always kept,
whether or not anyone was counting.
